# Module 1: The F.R.A.M.E. Method

The closing system that did $28M across five industries.

## The mindset you run on, start to finish

The more questions you ask, the more they tell you exactly how to sell them. Every buyer has a way they can be sold. It is buried in what they tell you, and the only way to dig it out is to ask and then actually listen.

So hold one question in the back of your mind from the first hello to the signature: how can this person be sold? You are not making small talk. You are running reconnaissance. The buyer will hand you the blueprint to their own decision if you ask enough good questions to get it.

And the line that runs through this entire system: **if they say it, they believe it. If you say it, they can doubt it.** Your job is to get them saying it.

## The five reasons anyone says no

Every no is one of these five. Name it and you can beat it.

1. No trust. They do not believe you yet.
2. No need. They do not feel the problem.
3. No interest. They checked out because you stopped involving them.
4. No hurry. The pain is not urgent.
5. No money. They do not see the payback.

Most reps hear "no money" and drop their price. Wrong. Four times out of five the real reason is one of the first four, and price is just the polite version. F.R.A.M.E. is built to remove all five before you ever ask for the sale.

## The three questions every buyer answers before they pay you

1. Why do I have to buy from you?
2. Why do I have to buy now?
3. Why should I pay more for this?

If your presentation answers all three, you do not need to push. They close themselves.

## The F.R.A.M.E. map

Five phases. Run them in order, every time.

### F · Frame the Room

Set the terms before they do. The frame is physical, mental, and emotional, and you win all three before you have said three real sentences.

The first person who has to believe you are the right person for this is you. Show up unbeatable. Disarm the resistance fast. Take control of the agenda. The classic disarm for the guarded, "just give me a price" buyer is to take the pressure off completely: "I do not even know if I can help you yet." You cannot talk price until you have seen everything.

If you sell in person, the seating is the most underrated weapon you have: both decision makers on the same side, facing you, your back to a wall, you control every sightline. If you sell on a call, the equivalent is the opening frame: you tell them how the conversation works and what the decision at the end will be.

Opening frame starter:
> "Here is how I run these. I am going to ask you some direct questions, you are going to be straight with me, and by the end we will both know if this is a fit. If it is, I will show you exactly what working together looks like. If not, I will tell you that too. Fair?"

### R · Read the Room

People buy from people they like, trust, and respect, and more than seventy percent of how they read you is nonverbal. Genuinely connect. Underneath the conversation you are quietly answering one question the whole time: how can this person be sold?

The one rule: ask more than you tell. Two ears, one mouth, in that ratio. You can be warm, funny, and personable and still be collecting the entire time. The line you never cross is selling here. Do not pitch your company, your product, or yourself in the warm up. Every answer is a clue. The detail they drop, the last vendor who burned them, the thing they are quietly worried about. That is them telling you how to close them.

Then confirm your buyer criteria before you invest the hour: they can actually buy, there is verifiable ability to pay, and every decision maker is present. If one is missing, surface it now, not at the close. Leaving a partner out is the single most common way reps lose a sold deal.

### A · Assess the Need

Become the expert and build the need so it is their need, in their words. By the end of this phase they should be thinking: I have a real problem and I need to deal with it now.

Run every question on **L.Q.R.: Listen, Question, Respond.** In that order, every time. They give you information, you pause and actually process it, you ask a layering question that goes one level deeper, you listen to that answer, and only then do you respond. Most reps run L.R., they listen and immediately respond. The layer is where the money is.

Layering questions that do the digging for you:
- "Tell me more about that."
- "Is that important to you? Why is that important to you?"
- "How long has that been going on?"
- "Would you want that handled for good?"
- "How would it feel to never think about that again?"

The core engine of this phase is **Problem to Cause to Trauma.** For every issue you uncover, walk them through three levels. Problem alone gets a shrug. Problem plus cause gets a nod. Problem plus cause plus trauma gets a decision. You are not closing here. You are making the need undeniable so the close has nothing left to fight. You are not rushing them. The problem is.

**[YOUR TURN]** Take your buyer's top 3 problems and write the Problem, the Cause, and the Trauma for each.

### M · Make the Market

Answer "why you," take them shopping across the whole market so they kill the cheap option themselves, and condition the price with a neutral source before you ever say your number.

First, build your authority. Raise their dissatisfaction with the industry, then build walls of value around you that no competitor can climb. Do not counter every negative with a positive about yourself. Let the dissatisfaction sit. The contrast does the selling. Most providers in any industry do not survive five years, so the real question is not just who does good work, it is who is still standing to honor the promise in year three.

Then take them shopping. Good, better, best, kept generic, so they reach your conclusion on their own. Kill the competition on quality, never on price.

Then condition the price with a neutral third party so your number never feels like it came from you. The frame that beats price every time is **expense versus investment.** Doing nothing is always the cheapest option today and the most expensive option tomorrow. By the time you finish this phase, doing nothing is off the table.

**[YOUR TURN]** Write your three authority "walls," your good-better-best tiers, and the neutral source or number you use to condition price.

### E · Earn the Yes

Present the price with total confidence, handle every objection with one repeatable system, give them a real reason to move today, and then make them sell the decision back to you. If you ran the first four phases clean, your price is a relief, not a shock.

The pre-close question isolates the real objection. Ask it, and if they say anything other than "no, it is just the money," you have a hidden objection. Find it, resolve it, and ask again, word for word, until the only thing left is price.

Present the price like it is the best news they will hear all week. Your demeanor does not change. Same energy, same smile you had during the demo. If you get tense at the price, they get tense at the price. Slow down. Rushing reads as fear.

The **S.A.L.E.S. objection engine.** Roughly ninety-nine percent of all objections are either time or money. Three rules during any objection: do not argue, do not attack their reasoning, and lead them to answer it themselves. Win the argument and you lose the sale.

Never drop your price before you validate value. A discount with no reason behind it reads as a game and creates zero urgency. Earn the right to the better number first. And if they still stall, the takeaway works: start to pack up, signal the presentation is over, and the real objection finally comes out.

## The Sell-It-Back: how every sale ends

This is the signature move. Do not skip it on a single deal. The moment they say yes, before the paperwork is done, you stop selling and you make them sell it. You hand the decision back and let them defend it in their own words.

Why? Because the words they say to you right now are the exact words they will say to their family, their partner, and the friend who asks "why didn't you just go with the cheap guy?" If they say it, they believe it.

Ask them why they decided to move forward, what made them comfortable, what they are most looking forward to. Then shut up and listen. Affirm it and lock it: "That is exactly right, and that is exactly why you made a smart call. Welcome." Now they have built their own story, out loud, and they own it.

Then lock it while the yes is warm. Money moves before the conversation ends. A yes is not a sale until the deposit clears.

M. Make the Market: authority, shop the market, condition the price.
E. Earn the Yes: present with confidence, run S.A.L.E.S., validate then move.
Sell-It-Back: make them say why, then lock the money.

# Module 2: How I Use AI to Produce Income

AI does not make you money. AI removes the time between you and the money. Every workflow below replaces hours you are either doing badly or not doing at all. Run them and you get more calls booked, more content shipped, and more deals closed with the same hours.

Four workflows. Each one is a system you set up once and reuse.

## Workflow 1: Outbound and lead generation

Goal: more qualified conversations booked, without you writing every message from blank.

1. Define your exact buyer in one sentence. Who they are, what they make, what breaks at their stage.
2. Build a research prompt that takes a name or company and returns the angle: their likely pain, a specific hook, one line of relevant context.
3. Build a message prompt that turns that research into a short, direct outreach message that sounds like you and asks for one thing: a call.
4. You review and send. AI drafts, you decide. Never fully automate the send.

Prompt starter:
> "You are my outbound assistant. My buyer: [buyer]. My offer: [offer]. Given this prospect [paste research], write a 4-sentence outreach message in a direct, no-fluff tone. One clear ask: book a call. No flattery, no hype."

**[YOUR TURN]** Paste your one-sentence buyer definition and your offer. Save your research prompt and your message prompt so you reuse the same two every day.

## Workflow 2: Content repurposing

Goal: one idea becomes a week of content across TikTok, IG, and the newsletter, so reach compounds instead of resetting.

1. Capture one real idea per day. A lesson from a call, a move that worked, a take you actually believe.
2. Feed it to AI with your formats: short-form hook and script, IG caption, newsletter angle.
3. Edit for your voice. AI gives you the skeleton, you add the part only you would say.
4. Ship daily. Volume plus consistency beats polish.

Prompt starter:
> "Here is one idea: [idea]. Turn it into: (1) a 30-second short-form script with a hook in the first line, (2) an IG caption, (3) a one-line newsletter angle. Tone: direct, operator energy, no hype, no clichés."

**[YOUR TURN]** List your three platforms and the format you want for each. Lock your tone instructions so every output sounds like you.

## Workflow 3: Call prep and follow-up

Goal: walk into every call prepared and out of every call with follow-up already drafted, so deals do not die in the gap.

1. Before the call: feed AI the prospect's context and get a prep sheet with likely objections and the questions to ask.
2. After the call: dump your notes and get a follow-up that references the specific things they said and restates the cost of inaction.
3. For stalled deals: get a short re-engagement message that adds a reason to move now, not just "checking in."

Prompt starter:
> "Prospect context: [notes]. My offer: [offer]. Give me: 3 likely objections and how to handle each on the S.A.L.E.S. rules, 5 layering questions, and one line that frames their cost of inaction as expense versus investment. After the call I will paste notes and you will write the follow-up."

**[YOUR TURN]** Save your prep prompt and your follow-up prompt. Feed in the objections from Module 1 so AI handles them your way.

## Workflow 4: Build the offer and the assets

Goal: spin up products, pages, and assets in hours instead of weeks. This product is the proof. You can build the next one the same way.

1. Outline the product as modules and outcomes first. What does the buyer walk away able to do.
2. Use AI to draft each section, then you cut, sharpen, and add your real scripts and numbers.
3. Use AI to draft the sales page, the delivery email, and the social posts that sell it.
4. Ship the rough version, sell it, improve it with buyer feedback. Do not wait for perfect.

Prompt starter:
> "I am building a [price] product called [name]. The buyer is [buyer]. Outcomes: [outcomes]. Draft the module structure, then draft section one. Tone: direct, no fluff. Leave clearly marked slots where I need to add my own scripts and numbers."
